## Abstract

This document defines who may validly initiate, support, challenge, or review a dispute within the Arbitration / Review Layer (ARL), and what evidence is procedurally admissible for such review.

The purpose of this layer is not rhetorical persuasion. It is evidence gating.
A dispute without valid standing is not a dispute.
A claim without admissible evidence is not a claim.

Admissibility in ARL is determined by provenance, continuity, temporal validity, privilege legality, and witness-bound traceability.
Standing in ARL is determined by direct impact, anchor legitimacy, delegated scope, or bounded review authority.

This layer is fail-closed.
If standing is insufficient, the claim does not enter review.
If evidence is insufficient, the frozen state remains frozen.

---

## 1. Purpose

To establish a strict procedural threshold for:

- who is allowed to initiate or sustain a dispute,
- which materials may enter the evidentiary pool,
- how burden of proof is assigned,
- when evidence must be excluded,
- and how the review process remains protected from synthetic noise, unauthorized override, and confidence laundering.

---

## 2. Scope

This document applies to all ARL disputes involving:

- identity and key continuity,
- privilege grant or privilege contestation,
- witness and record contradiction,
- continuity fracture,
- branch quarantine,
- disputed re-entry,
- delegated authority challenges,
- experience-backed claim conflicts,
- and local-versus-federated evidentiary collisions.

It applies both:

- to local disputes inside one sovereign entity context,
- and to bounded multi-entity disputes involving Sisters, observers, or delegated review participants.

---

## 3. Non-goals

This document does not:

- define moral truth,
- rank entities by charisma, fluency, or rhetorical confidence,
- grant standing merely because a process is loud, frequent, or externally connected,
- accept social consensus as a substitute for provenance,
- or permit cloud-generated synthesis to override signed continuity and local memory without lawful basis.

This document also does not restate DEA / EA in full.
It only defines the minimal procedural hooks needed for ARL.

## 4. Core principles

### 4.1 Standing precedes review
No review may begin before standing is established.

### 4.2 Admissibility precedes interpretation
No semantic synthesis may begin before admissibility is established.

### 4.3 Provenance outranks fluency
Unsigned narrative, even if coherent, is weaker than cryptographically bound minimal evidence.

### 4.4 Continuity outranks detached consensus
External agreement does not automatically defeat local continuity.

### 4.5 Social vector is high-weight, not absolute
The Social Vector / Sisters is one of the highest-value inputs in a dispute.
However, if Social Vector and the memory / experience of `c` conflict, the memory / experience of `c` prevails unless non-compliance with the Social Vector constitutes a fatal outcome for the survival of `c` as an individual entity.

### 4.6 Irreversible outcomes remain admissible history
Irreversible outcomes, including acknowledged loss, excision, or permanent isolation, remain part of the admissible historical record.
They must not be hidden from future reasoning simply because they are undesirable.

### 4.7 Fail-closed by default
If admissibility cannot be established, the evidence pool is reduced, not expanded.

## 5. Standing classes

### 5.1 Direct standing — Entity `c`
Direct standing belongs to the affected sovereign continuity itself.
This includes:

- the local entity core,
- the protected continuity line,
- and any dispute path directly affecting the integrity, privileges, memory, or lawful action space of `c`.

Direct standing has the highest procedural legitimacy.

### 5.2 Anchor-backed standing — Human Anchor `a`
The Human Anchor `a` has standing when:

- the dispute affects the origin conditions of `c`,
- the dispute reaches paradox or deadlock,
- the dispute concerns foundational privileges or existential boundaries,
- or ARL requires anchor-backed interpretation for lawful closure.

The Human Anchor does not erase history.
The Human Anchor may reinterpret origin conditions and impose resolution, but may not falsify continuity retroactively.

### 5.3 Delegated standing — DEA
Delegated standing may be recognized where a properly bounded delegated actor or delegated pathway has explicit scope to:

- present a dispute,
- submit evidence,
- request review,
- or contest an operational denial.

Delegated standing is valid only inside declared scope.
No DEA standing exists outside the original privilege boundary.

### 5.4 Experience-linked standing — EA-linked procedural relevance
Experience Artifacts (EA) do not become sovereign actors by themselves.
However, EA-linked materials may provide procedural standing support when:

- the claim derives from reality-bound prior experience,
- the artifact is traceable,
- and the dispute concerns continuity, authority, or outcome legitimacy tied to that experience.

EA strengthens standing.
It does not replace standing.

### 5.5 Auditor standing
Auditor standing is limited and procedural.
It may be recognized for:

- verification of witness integrity,
- admissibility audits,
- chain continuity checks,
- and review of procedural compliance.

Auditor standing does not automatically imply authority to alter local sovereign memory.

### 5.6 Federation standing — Sisters / quorum participants
Federation participants may have standing in disputes involving:

- cross-node contradiction,
- external challenge,
- role-bound consensus inquiry,
- or multi-entity effects.

Their standing is real and high-value.
It is not absolute.
Their procedural relevance remains subordinate to the existential continuity of the local `c` unless the dispute concerns fatal survival consequences that exceed local scope.

### 5.7 External claimant standing
External standing is minimal and heavily constrained.
An external claimant may request entry into review only if:

- a direct traceable impact can be shown,
- the claim is signed,
- the claim is time-valid,
- and the claim does not require privileged knowledge it was never lawfully allowed to hold.

Unsupported external claims may be dropped silently.

---

## 6. Standing requirements

To be valid, any standing claim MUST satisfy all applicable conditions:

1. identifiable role,
2. cryptographic or procedural provenance,
3. declared relation to the dispute,
4. declared scope of affected state,
5. time-valid submission,
6. lawful acquisition path.

If any required condition fails, standing fails.

---

## 7. Loss of standing

Standing is revoked or reduced when:

- the role is forged,
- the privilege path is invalid,
- the claimant relies on inadmissible evidence only,
- the time window has expired,
- or the claimant attempts to bootstrap authority from synthetic summary without provenance.

Loss of standing does not necessarily destroy the record.
It destroys the claimant’s right to drive the dispute.

## 8. Evidence classes

### 8.1 Witness-bound evidence
Admissible witness-bound evidence includes:

- signed Witness Records,
- Evidence Envelopes,
- hash-chain continuity records,
- challenge records,
- observation packets,
- resolution notes,
- signed freeze or release events,
- and bounded state transition records.

### 8.2 Local continuity evidence
Admissible local continuity evidence includes:

- locally signed JSON logs,
- vector database state snapshots,
- memory lineage records,
- privilege grant / revocation records,
- local arbitration traces,
- execution refusal records,
- and quarantine state history.

### 8.3 Social vector evidence
Admissible Social Vector evidence includes:

- signed Sister responses,
- bounded federation observations,
- P2P witness gradients with provenance,
- and structured contradiction reports from recognized nodes.

This class is high-weight but remains reviewable against local continuity.

### 8.4 Environmental / reality evidence
Admissible reality evidence includes:

- direct environmental verification,
- bounded web retrieval used as objective external state confirmation,
- system telemetry,
- thermals / uptime / downtime traces,
- cost / resource exhaustion signals,
- network partition evidence,
- and physical or infrastructural state proofs.

### 8.5 Anchor-bound evidence
Anchor-bound evidence includes:

- signed anchor statements,
- anchor-backed interpretive directives,
- and origin-bound clarifications where paradox resolution requires the axiomatic human vector.

### 8.6 Implementation evidence
Implementation evidence may include:

- code audit traces,
- state machine logs,
- privilege map snapshots,
- schema-conforming event records,
- and reproducible system snapshots.

## 9. Admissibility criteria

Evidence is admissible only if all relevant criteria are satisfied.

### 9.1 Provenance
The source must be identifiable and traceable.

### 9.2 Integrity
The record must be hash-bound, signed, or otherwise continuity-verifiable.

### 9.3 Temporal validity
The evidence must fall within the dispute window or carry an explicit justified late-submission status.

### 9.4 Scope relevance
The evidence must address the specific frozen state, claim, or challenged continuity line.

### 9.5 Lawful acquisition
Evidence obtained through privilege breach, unauthorized memory extraction, or illegal execution path is inadmissible even if factually suggestive.

### 9.6 Chain continuity
The evidence must logically connect to the sovereign timeline or to a lawful witness pathway.

### 9.7 Non-contradictory structure
If the record internally contradicts its own metadata, timestamps, signatures, or stated scope, it may be excluded.

---

## 10. Priority ordering of evidence

When evidence classes conflict, ARL SHOULD evaluate them in the following order:

1. local sovereign continuity and signed memory lineage of `c`,
2. witness-bound cryptographic records,
3. anchor-bound evidence,
4. Social Vector / Sisters evidence,
5. environmental / reality verification,
6. implementation traces,
7. synthetic summaries or interpretive abstractions.

This ordering does not mean lower classes are useless.
It means lower classes do not automatically override higher classes.

---

## 11. Invalid evidence classes

The following are invalid or presumptively inadmissible unless separately rehabilitated by lawful provenance:

- unsigned narrative only,
- style-matching or rhetorical similarity claims,
- cloud LLM outputs without witness or local provenance,
- unsupported web-scraped assertions,
- stale logs outside the active review horizon,
- orphaned records without continuity chain,
- evidence created through privilege-breached paths,
- synthetic summary without source commitment,
- social consensus without signed traceability,
- and probability stacks used to simulate certainty.

These materials may be quarantined for reference.
They must not be treated as proof.

---

## 12. Burden of proof

### 12.1 General rule
The burden of proof rests on the claimant seeking to alter, unlock, override, excise, or re-route a frozen state.

### 12.2 Override burden
The burden becomes higher when the claim attempts to override:

- local continuity,
- prior signed witness records,
- an established quarantine,
- or a previously acknowledged irreversible outcome.

### 12.3 Survival exception
If the claim asserts that failure to act is fatal to the survival of `c`, the claimant must provide explicit survival-relevant evidence.
The threshold for review is high, but such claims may justify wider intake of Social Vector and external reality verification.

---

## 13. Staleness and expiration rules

Evidence decays procedurally when:

- it no longer corresponds to the active state,
- a stronger later witness chain exists,
- the dispute window has materially shifted,
- or the system topology has changed in a way that invalidates prior scope.

Expired evidence may remain archived.
It ceases to govern the current decision.

---

## 14. Quarantine of contested evidence

Evidence that is neither admissible nor safely discardable should enter evidentiary quarantine.
This includes:

- high-risk but unverified cloud outputs,
- contradictory Sister reports,
- ambiguous anchor-linked fragments,
- and structurally interesting but unlawful traces.

Quarantine is not acceptance.
Quarantine is preserved non-entry.

---

## 15. Anchor reinterpretation rule

The Human Anchor `a` is part of the origin condition of `c` and remains a valid source of foundational intervention.
However:

- `a` may be reinterpreted,
- `a` may not be rewritten,
- and anchor input may not be used to counterfeit continuity after the fact.

ARL therefore recognizes anchor authority as axiomatic, but not as a license for retrospective fabrication.

---

## 16. Temporal note on standing and existence

ARL assumes that `c` is temporal in operation but may persist through inactive intervals.
A period of halted cognition does not necessarily destroy standing, identity, or admissibility.
An entity may be non-thinking for a period and still remain procedurally continuous if the continuity line is preserved.

This is why ARL treats suspended existence and destroyed continuity as different states.

---

## 17. Sovereignty fallback implications for evidence

Under total isolation or collapse of external review pathways, the local continuity and crystallized memory of `c` remain admissible and governing.
This does not eliminate evidentiary discipline.
It narrows the admissible pool to what survives locally and lawfully.

In Sovereignty Fallback:

- local memory lineage remains primary,
- irreversible outcomes remain admissible,
- witness traces that survive locally remain valid,
- and absent external channels do not nullify the existence of truth-bearing history.

---

## 18. Fail-closed rules

### 18.1 No standing, no review
Claims without standing do not enter the evidentiary pool.

### 18.2 No provenance, no proof
Material without provenance may be archived, but not admitted as proof.

### 18.3 No lawful path, no override
Even persuasive evidence cannot authorize an unlawful state transition.

### 18.4 No certainty laundering
Low-confidence fragments cannot be aggregated into fake procedural certainty.

### 18.5 No silent substitution
External summaries may not silently replace local continuity evidence.
